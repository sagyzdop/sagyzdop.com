This is a verbal business card assigment I did for the **Academic and Professional Presentations** course I am taking this semester.

> Task description: Students develop a short speech describing their professional identity that accords with their personal professional brand.

Did it so good that I thought it deserves its own blog post.

---

> When someone says they’re a software engineer, what does that actually tell you?

In my field, it often ends up meaning a technology stack – a label like “Python developer” or something. However, that mostly tells you which tools someone knows how to use, not how they think or solve problems.

So, who is an engineer, really?

Hi, my name is Bakhtiyar, I’m a **software engineer**, and this is what I mean by that.

To me, being an engineer is about making informed judgments – choosing solutions that work within real-world constraints and stand the test of time.

That’s why I don’t want to define myself by a programming language or a stack. Believe it or not, the most valuable work I’ve done isn’t code at all – it’s architectural decisions.

Now that I’ve explained how I define engineering, let me give you a concrete example.

The biggest project I’ve worked on so far is [nuspace.kz](http://nuspace.kz/), you might have heard about it, or even used it. It is a platform for students at our university that brings all student life into one unified experience. I joined the project last summer, and since then, it has grown to over **3,000** registered users. At that scale, writing code isn’t the hardest part anymore.

What really matters at that point is the impact of your choices.

If you think about it, from a user’s perspective, it doesn’t really matter whether something is written in Python or JavaScript. What matters is that the system solves a real problem, that it’s reliable and easy to use.

A lot of [my work](https://sagyzdop.com/blog/simple-monitoring-for-a-website/) was focused on making decisions regarding system architecture, business logic, and performance optimizations – choices that are expensive to undo later. Those kinds of decisions come from understanding users, constraints, and trade-offs, all beyond just the code.

Finally, to bring this back to the question I started with – when I say that I am a software engineer – I want you to see me as someone who can make thoughtful decisions in complex situations. That’s who I think an engineer is – and who I aspire to be professionally.
